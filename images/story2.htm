Awesome Eggs

“Full of protein, healthy vitamins and minerals, the egg is making a diet comeback.”

Eggs are a delicious and healthy component to any diet, providing protein, omega-3 fats and essential vitamins and minerals. Here are a few reasons why you should include eggs in your diet.

1

Vitamins and Minerals

Vitamins and Minerals

Eggs are a good source of many important vitamins and minerals. A three-egg omelet has almost a third of your daily recommended dose of vitamin B12 as well as healthy doses of vitamin A, riboflavin, selenium and a variety of other nutrients.

2

(Good) Fat for Breakfast

(Good) Fat for Breakfast

Studies have shown that consuming fat first thing in the morning primes the metabolism to break it down during the rest of the day. The fat in eggs can set you up for metabolic success the rest of the day. 

source
2/15
3

Full of Protein

Full of Protein

Protein is essential for maintaining the structures of the body and is especially crucial for those looking to gain muscle and strength. Every whole egg has 6 grams of protein and all 8 essential amino acids. The protein in eggs is of such high quality that it's often used by scientists as a standard to judge the biological value of other proteins.

source
3/15
4

Omega-3 Eggs

Omega-3 Eggs

Chickens fed a special diet may lay eggs that contain higher amounts of omega-3 fats than normal eggs. Omega-3 fats have been shown to lower blood triglycerides and improve heart health and are normally only found in significant quantities in seafood. The amount of omega-3 fats varies greatly by brand so read labels carefully.

source
4/15
5

Vitamin D

Vitamin D

Egg yolks are one of the few foods that naturally contain vitamin D. It's an essential nutrient that's tough to find in food sources (especially if you don't eat fish), which is why milk and dairy foods are often fortified with it. To prevent osteoporosis and other negative health outcomes, don't skimp on egg yolks.

source
5/15
6

Heart Healthy

Heart Healthy

Eggs are high in cholesterol and we all know high blood cholesterol is bad for the heart, so we shouldn't eat eggs, right? Wrong. The cholesterol we eat actually has little impact on the cholesterol in our blood. The real health boogeymen appears to be the trans fats found in processed foods. Guess how much trans fat is found in eggs? None. 

source
6/15
7

Healthy Carotenoids

Healthy Carotenoids

Recent studies have shown that consuming eggs with raw vegetables increases the absorption of carotenoids like beta carotene, alpha carotene, lycopene, lutein and zeaxanthin. Carotenoids help to prevent health risks including cancer and eye disease. Throw a few hard-boiled eggs on top of your next salad to turn those vegetables into instant superfoods.

source
7/15
8

Mix in Vegetables

Mix in Vegetables

Studies continue to show that Americans are not eating enough vegetables. Our friend the egg can help out. Omelets and frittatas are great for sneaking extra vegetables into your diet, especially in the morning when many people don't consume vegetables at all. Throw whatever you have on hand (peppers, spinach and onions work well) in with your eggs to increase your daily vegetable consumption.

source
8/15
9

Dark Yolks Have Extra Vitamins

Dark Yolks Have Extra Vitamins

The yolks of some eggs have a hue that is darker and more orange than traditional eggs. This is caused by the hen's diet and is usually present in pasture-raised chickens that eat more foraged seeds and bugs and less manufactured feed. Eggs with darker yolks often have higher levels of vitamin A and healthy omega-3 fats.

source
9/15
10

Budget Friendly

Budget Friendly

Despite recent price hikes, eggs are still among the best values in the grocery store. A dozen eggs can often be had for under $3 and can provide multiple meals for an individual, something you can't usually say about the equivalent amount of fish, chicken or beef. For those on a budget, eggs are a good protein source.

source
10/15
11

Eggs to Feel Full

Eggs to Feel Full

Egg-based meals have been shown to make people feel full longer than meals composed of other foods with similar calorie levels. Eating eggs with breakfast or lunch will help you avoid reaching for that cookie two hours later or losing your willpower and ordering a pizza for your next meal.

source
11/15
12

Eggs for Hair and Skin

Eggs for Hair and Skin

Eggs are the best food source of keratin in terms of bioavailability. Keratin is a key component for hair, nail, and skin health and growth. Eggs are also good sources of sulphur and vitamin B12, other nutrients key to good hair growth.

12/15
13

Eggs for Breakfast, Lose Weight

Eggs for Breakfast, Lose Weight

Skipping breakfast has been shown to add inches to your waistline. Eating protein for breakfast also lowers cortisol, a hormone that increases fat deposits. Minimize cortisol by consuming 20 to 30 grams of protein in the morning, the amount found in four to five eggs.

source
13/15
14

Nerve and Eye Health

Nerve and Eye Health

Eggs are high in B12, as well as lutein and zeaxanthin which help to prevent eye degeneration. Nutritionist Amanda Ursell told Men's Health, "B12 is essential for forming the protective coating around the nerves. ... A deficiency can lead to irreversible nerve damage."

source
14/15
15

Egg Variety

Egg Variety

We live in an age of egg diversity. In addition to the normal white and brown eggs, you can get free-range, organic and omega-3-fortified eggs in almost any supermarket. Eggs from quails, ducks and heritage breeds of chickens can also be found at farmers' markets and specialty stores. While the jury is still out on whether or not the varieties taste different, the varying look of the shells and yolks can be a pleasant way to mix up your egg game.

source
15/15
